Geospatial Analysis of Voter Turnout in London's Local Elections

In my second-year Geocomputation coursework, I analysed the geographic distribution of voter turnout in London’s local elections. Here are some key findings from the project:

London's Divided Political Participation

Hotspot and coldspot areas were identified using the Getis-Ord Gi* statistic, with high voter turnout clustering in the southwest boroughs such as Richmond upon Thames and Kingston upon Thames, and low turnout clustering in areas such as Barking and Dagenham and central Southwark. There are intra-borough disparities, such as an east-west divide within Haringey, with higher turnout in the western area and lower in the eastern area.

Polling Station Proximity ≠ Turnout

Since the locations of polling stations from 2018 are not publicly available, I used the dataset from the 2010 elections in North London, which is available here. The addresses of polling stations were converted into geographic coordinates using the Google Maps Geocoding API.

Using DBSCAN (Density-Based Spatial Clustering of Applications with Noise), I overlaid the identified clusters on a choropleth of in-person voter turnout. Interestingly, areas within the DBSCAN-identified clusters tended to have lower voter turnout, while northern wards with higher turnout were often outside these clusters.

Does Access to Public Transit Really Drive Voter Turnout?

Accessibility was measured by calculating the shortest path distance between polling stations and nearby public transit (bus, tube, and train) using OpenStreetMap API. The Borough of Enfield, which had the highest in-person turnout in North London, also had a relatively low concentration of polling stations.

Although central Enfield wards with high turnout also had good accessibility, the relationship was not always consistent. For example, Edmonton Green had excellent accessibility but a relatively low turnout, whereas Chase had limited accessibility but a high turnout. So, the answer to the question is no simple yes or no; voter behaviour is undeniably complex and determined by multifaceted factors beyond what we can capture through spatial analysis.

Revisiting the Project 2 Years Later

Looking back on this project in 2025, I recognise limitations in the "timeliness" of the data I've used. My project was completed just before the Greater London Authority released the 2022 local election data, and the lack of a centralised and publicly accessible database for polling station locations was a huge challenge. Not many local councils publish past polling locations, which led me to use data from the 2010 and 2018 local elections - neither of which captures the volatile, socially and politically evolving unique characteristics of London.

Although this project met the requirements of the coursework, with the benefit of hindsight and greater freedom, I would be interested in conducting a longitudinal study examining changes in voter turnout across London from 2010 to 2025. This would illuminate interesting insights into where political participation has increased, where it has declined, and how these trends correlate with demographic shifts, gentrification, and broader socioeconomic change.
