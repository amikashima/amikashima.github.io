Hostility or Hospitality? MPs' Attitudes Towards Refugees in the UK Parliament

The escalation in asylum caseloads in the UK has catalysed heated political debates over the fairness of the asylum system. To systematically analyse political rhetoric and attitudes toward refugees, I applied NLP methods to speech transcipts from the House of Commons between 2015 and 2023 using data from Hansard. Here are my three key findings from my undergraduate dissertation.

1. Event-Driven Empathy and Hostility

My analysis revealed erratic, event-driven fluctuations in expressions of “hostility” and “hospitality”. For example, there was a very notable surge in hospitable sentiment following the arrival of Ukrainian refugees, contrasted with a spike in hostility toward those seeking protection through “illegal” means. Speech sentiment classification was performed using supervised machine learning (via scikit-learn), trained on a dataset of 600 manually labelled speeches.

2. Divided or United?

Perhaps the most compelling finding from my study is the partisan divergence and convergence in sentiments across the studied time period. For instance, during the introduction of the Illegal Migration Bill, the predominant expressions were polarised between Conservative MPs, who had more expressions of hostility, and MPs from major opposition parties, who had dominant expressions of hospitality. However, during discussions over Syrian refugees in September 2015 and Ukrainian refugees in March 2022, there was a unified dominance in hospitable sentiment across most parties, indicating a relative cross-party agreement on the "deservingness" for these refugees to receive protection in the UK.

3. Rebellion Within Party

What makes NLP on parliamentary speeches especially interesting is that MPs have a level of freedom to dissent from party affiliations and express disapproval. This creates an opportunity to study intra-party politics; that is, disparities and disagreements in policy positions within a single political party. These nuances are not evident in UK Parliament voting records, where MPs typically vote in alignment with party directives, regardless of their personal positions. I used Wordscore from Quanteda to estimate MPs’ ideological positions along a left–right spectrum, which captured more "far-leaning" politicians who are more hostile or sympathetic in their rhetorics.
