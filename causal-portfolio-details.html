Causal Impact of the 2018 Voter ID Pilot Scheme on Voter Turnout in London

In 2018, the Voter ID Pilot Scheme required voters in the Borough of Bromley (Fig 1) to present photographic ID at polling stations, a controversial policy which raised concerns surrounding potential voter suppression and disenfranchisement. Official reports concluded that there was “no evidence” that this scheme significantly affected turnout based on exploratory analyses. My study aimed to reassess this claim using difference-in-differences as a causal inference framework:

Difference-in-differences Methodology

I constructed a panel dataset using ward-level turnout data from 2014, 2016, and 2018 local and mayoral elections. To improve comparability, I combined difference-in-differences with Propensity Score Matching (PSM) by pairing Bromley’s 22 treated wards with 85 control wards matched on sociodemographic and political variables (Fig 2). I also controlled for time-varying covariates like population age structure and competitiveness of elections. The model was validated using parallel trend checks and a placebo DiD test.

Estimated Effect of Voter ID Requirements

Voter turnout declined by 5.7 percentage points in treated wards between 2016 and 2018, compared to 3.6 points in matched control wards. The DiD model estimated a 2.29 percentage point reduction in turnout due to the ID requirement, but this effect was not statistically significant (p = 0.19). Although the result does not confirm voter suppression due to voter ID requirements, it is important to note that Bromley is a predominantly white and relatively conservative population in London - these are important demographic and political features that may have underestimated the policy’s effects on turnout.

Future Directions

Ideally, future studies should estimate the subgroup-specific impacts (e.g. by age or ethnicity), especially for higher-stakes elections like the General Election where more diverse population participate. Although granular voter participation data is not publicly available, such studies can help us understand who might be most affected by voter ID policies and guide us towards more equitable electoral reforms.
